2. QRCode Monkey

Best for: Branded QR codes with logos and custom designs.

The most customizable free QR generator available. Embed a logo (PNG/SVG), choose dot shapes (rounded, dots, squares), set gradient colors, and add frames with call-to-action text. SVG/PDF/EPS export for print. Truly free with no watermarks.

Pros: Logo embedding, custom dot shapes, gradient colors, high-res export (SVG/PDF/EPS), no watermarks, no limits.

Cons: Server-side generation (your URL is sent to their API), no scan tracking on free tier, ads.

3. GoQR.me

Best for: Quick generation with direct API access for developers.

Clean tool with a useful API: https://api.qrserver.com/v1/create-qr-code/?size=300x300&data=YourURL. The API is free and requires no authentication — useful for generating QR codes programmatically in scripts or apps.

Pros: Free API, SVG export, custom colors, clean interface.

Cons: Server-side, no logo embedding, limited customization compared to QRCode Monkey.

4. QR Code Generator (qr-code-generator.com)

Best for: Businesses that need dynamic QR codes (editable destination URL after printing).

Free tier generates basic static QR codes. The real product is their paid plan ($5/month) for dynamic QR codes — you can change where the QR code points after it's been printed, and track scans. Most customization features are paywalled.

Pros: Dynamic QR codes (paid), scan analytics (paid), high-res downloads (paid).

Cons: Most features are paid, free tier is very basic (PNG only, no colors), heavy upselling.

Generates QR codes client-side as you type, with live preview. Extremely minimal — no color options, no formats, just a QR code you can right-click to save. Perfect when you literally just need a QR code and nothing else.

Pros: Client-side, instant live preview, zero friction, no account.

Static vs Dynamic QR Codes

Static QR codes encode the destination directly. The URL is baked into the QR pattern. They work forever, even offline, and can't be tracked or changed after printing. This is what all free generators create.

Dynamic QR codes encode a redirect URL (e.g., qr.example.com/abc123) that forwards to your actual destination. You can change the destination after printing and track scan analytics. These always require a paid service because someone has to run the redirect server.

Tip: If you're printing QR codes on physical materials (menus, posters, product packaging), consider whether the destination URL might change. If it might, either use a URL shortener you control or invest in dynamic QR codes. Reprinting physical materials is expensive.

Error Correction Levels

QR codes have built-in error correction — they can still be scanned even when partially damaged or obscured. The four levels are:

  • L (Low — 7%): Smallest QR code, least redundancy. Use for digital-only codes that won't be damaged.
  • M (Medium — 15%): Good default. Handles minor smudges and scratches.
  • Q (Quartile — 25%): Use for printed materials that might get worn.
  • H (High — 30%): Required when embedding a logo over part of the QR code. The logo obscures ~10-15% of the pattern, so high error correction compensates.
